Debt Collector Harassment

Know the difference between aggressive collection and illegal harassment under federal law

What Counts as Harassment

The FDCPA prohibits debt collector harassment under Section 1692d. Illegal harassment includes calling repeatedly with intent to annoy, using obscene or profane language, threatening violence or harm, publishing lists of consumers who refuse to pay, and calling without identifying themselves as debt collectors.

Courts have found harassment in as few as 3-4 calls per day or when collectors call at unreasonable hours despite being told to stop. The key factor is whether the collector's behavior would be considered abusive by a reasonable person.

Documenting Harassment

Keep a detailed log of every call: date, time, caller ID number, name of collector, what was said, and your emotional response. Save all voicemails -- do not delete them. Screenshot any text messages. Keep all letters and emails. This documentation is critical if you file a complaint or lawsuit.

If your state allows one-party consent recording, consider recording all collector calls. In two-party consent states, announce at the start of each call that you are recording. This often causes collectors to immediately improve their behavior.

Filing Complaints

Report harassment to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (consumerfinance.gov), your state attorney general, and the Federal Trade Commission. These agencies track patterns and can take enforcement action against repeat offenders.

You can also sue the collector directly for statutory damages of up to $1,000 plus actual damages and attorney fees. Many consumer protection attorneys handle these cases on contingency.

The Nuclear Option: Bankruptcy

If harassment is coming from multiple collectors on multiple debts, bankruptcy eliminates them all at once. The automatic stay immediately prohibits all collector contact, and any collector who violates the stay faces sanctions. After discharge, the discharge injunction permanently bars future collection attempts.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many calls per day is considered harassment?

There is no specific number, but courts have found harassment in as few as 3-4 calls per day, especially when the consumer has asked the collector to stop calling.

Can collectors contact my family or employer?

Collectors can contact third parties only to obtain your contact information, and cannot reveal that you owe a debt. They generally cannot contact your employer except to verify employment or garnish wages under a court order.

What should I say when a harassing collector calls?

Tell them the call is being documented, request they communicate only in writing, and send a cease and desist letter by certified mail.

Can I block collector phone numbers?

Yes, but this does not solve the underlying debt. The collector may call from different numbers or escalate to legal action. Addressing the debt through validation, negotiation, or bankruptcy is more effective.
