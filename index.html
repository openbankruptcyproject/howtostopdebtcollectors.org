How to Stop Debt Collectors

Your FDCPA Rights and Practical Steps to End Harassment

Know Your Rights

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) gives you powerful rights against debt collectors. They cannot call before 8 AM or after 9 PM, threaten you, use profane language, call your workplace after being told not to, contact your friends or family about your debt, or misrepresent what you owe.

Step 1: Request Debt Validation

Within 30 days of a collector's first contact, send a written request for debt validation. The collector must provide: the amount of the debt, the name of the original creditor, and proof you owe it. They must stop collection until they provide validation. Many collectors cannot produce adequate documentation.

Step 2: Send a Cease and Desist Letter

You can legally demand the collector stop all contact. Send the letter via certified mail. After receiving it, the collector can only contact you to confirm they will stop or to notify you of a lawsuit. Cease and desist template and guide.

Step 3: Document Everything

Keep a call log with dates, times, and what was said. Save every voicemail and letter. Screenshot text messages. This documentation is evidence if you decide to file an FDCPA lawsuit.

Step 4: File Complaints

CFPB: consumerfinance.gov/complaint. Your state Attorney General's office. FTC: reportfraud.ftc.gov. These agencies track collector behavior and can take enforcement action.

The Permanent Solution: Bankruptcy

Cease and desist letters stop calls. Bankruptcy stops the debt. The discharge injunction makes any future collection a federal court violation -- not just an FDCPA violation, but contempt of court. How discharge works.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a debt collector sue me?

Yes. A cease and desist letter stops calls but does not prevent a lawsuit. If you are at risk of being sued, consider bankruptcy.

What damages can I recover under the FDCPA?

Up to $1,000 in statutory damages per lawsuit, plus actual damages (emotional distress, lost wages) and attorney fees.

Does the FDCPA apply to all collectors?

It applies to third-party debt collectors and debt buyers. Original creditors are generally not covered, though some state laws provide similar protections.

How do I stop debt collectors from calling?

Send a written cease-and-desist letter via certified mail. Under the FDCPA, once a collector receives your written request to stop contact, they must stop calling -- except to confirm they will stop or to notify you of a specific legal action. Keep a copy of the letter and the certified mail receipt as proof.

What are my rights under the FDCPA?

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act prohibits collectors from: calling before 8 AM or after 9 PM, using threats or abusive language, contacting you at work if told not to, discussing your debt with third parties, misrepresenting the amount owed, and failing to validate the debt when requested. Violations can result in statutory damages of up to $1,000 per lawsuit.

Can I sue a debt collector?

Yes. If a debt collector violates the FDCPA, you can sue in federal or state court within one year of the violation. You can recover actual damages (emotional distress, lost wages), statutory damages up to $1,000, and attorney fees. Many consumer attorneys take FDCPA cases on contingency, meaning no upfront cost to you.
