What Debt Collectors Cannot Legally Do

15 things debt collectors are prohibited from doing under federal and state law

Prohibited Practices Under the FDCPA

The FDCPA creates a detailed list of prohibited practices. Collectors cannot threaten arrest or criminal prosecution (debt is civil, not criminal). They cannot claim to be attorneys or government officials when they are not. They cannot add unauthorized fees or interest to the balance. They cannot deposit post-dated checks before the date written.

Collectors also cannot contact you at inconvenient times or places. The law presumes that calls before 8am or after 9pm are inconvenient. They cannot contact you at work if you tell them your employer prohibits such calls. They cannot discuss your debt with anyone other than you, your spouse, your attorney, or a co-signer.

False and Misleading Representations

Section 1692e prohibits 16 specific types of false representations. Collectors cannot imply they are affiliated with the government, misrepresent the legal status of the debt, threaten actions they cannot legally take (like suing on time-barred debt), or falsely claim the debt is a crime. They cannot send documents that falsely appear to be from a court or government agency.

Any collector who tells you that you will be arrested, that they will garnish your wages without a court order (for regular debts), or that failing to pay is a criminal offense is violating the FDCPA.

Unfair Practices

Section 1692f prohibits unfair collection practices. Collectors cannot collect amounts not authorized by the original agreement or law. They cannot take or threaten to take property when they have no legal right to do so. They cannot cause charges (like collect call fees) to be billed to you by concealing the purpose of a communication.

Perhaps most importantly in the context of zombie debt, collectors cannot sue or threaten to sue on debt that they know is past the statute of limitations.

State Law Additions

Many states add additional protections beyond the FDCPA. Some states prohibit collectors from contacting consumers by text message or social media without consent. Others require collectors to be licensed in the state and maintain surety bonds. Check your state attorney general's website for state-specific restrictions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a debt collector threaten to arrest me?

No. Debt is a civil matter, not criminal. Threatening arrest or criminal prosecution for unpaid consumer debt violates the FDCPA.

Can collectors call my cell phone?

Yes, but if you did not give the collector your cell number, robocalls to your cell may violate the Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA), which provides for $500-$1,500 per call in damages.

Can collectors sue me on old debt?

They can attempt to, but suing on debt past the statute of limitations may violate the FDCPA. Check your state deadlines at debtstatuteoflimitations.com.

Can collectors add fees to my balance?

Only if the original agreement or applicable law specifically authorizes the fees. Unauthorized fees violate the FDCPA.
