The FDCPA Protects You

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (15 U.S.C. 1692) is the primary federal law governing how debt collectors can treat you. It applies to third-party collectors and debt buyers - not original creditors collecting their own debts. The law restricts when and how collectors can contact you, what they can say, and what actions they can take.

Under the FDCPA, collectors cannot call before 8am or after 9pm, cannot contact you at work if told to stop, cannot use profanity or threats, cannot misrepresent the amount owed, and must send written notice of the debt within five days of first contact. Violations carry statutory damages of up to $1,000 per lawsuit plus actual damages and attorney fees.

Your Right to Dispute

Within 30 days of first contact, you can dispute the debt in writing. The collector must then stop all collection activity until they verify the debt. This is one of the most powerful consumer protections in the FDCPA, especially for old debts where documentation may be incomplete. For a ready-to-use letter template, visit debtvalidationletter.org.

Cease and Desist

You have the right to tell a collector to stop contacting you entirely by sending a written cease and desist letter. Under Section 1692c(c), once the collector receives your letter, they can only contact you to confirm they will stop or to notify you of specific legal action. They cannot call, write, or contact third parties about your debt. Get a template at ceaseanddesistdebt.com.

When to Sue the Collector

If a collector violates the FDCPA, you can sue them in state or federal court within one year of the violation. Many consumer protection attorneys take these cases on contingency because the FDCPA provides for fee-shifting. Document every interaction - keep a log of calls, save all letters and voicemails, and record conversations if your state allows it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the FDCPA?

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act - a federal law that restricts how third-party debt collectors can treat consumers. It prohibits harassment, misrepresentation, and unfair practices.

Can I tell a collector to stop calling?

Yes. Send a written cease and desist letter. After receiving it, the collector can only contact you to confirm they will stop or to notify you of legal action.

What damages can I recover for FDCPA violations?

Up to $1,000 in statutory damages per lawsuit, plus actual damages (emotional distress, lost wages) and attorney fees.

Does the FDCPA apply to original creditors?

No. The FDCPA applies to third-party debt collectors and debt buyers. Original creditors collecting their own debts are generally not covered, though state laws may provide similar protections.
